Why Do Pet Medicine Prices Vary So Much in the UK?

If your dog or cat's repeat prescription seems to cost more every time, you're not imagining it — and new 2026 rules mean you now have more power to do something about it.

If you've ever picked up a repeat prescription for your dog or cat and wondered why the same medicine seemed to cost more this time than last, you're not imagining things — and you're far from alone.

Rising veterinary costs have become one of the biggest financial worries for UK pet owners. According to PDSA's Pet Health Inequality Report, over 1 in 5 UK pet owners now say pet ownership is more expensive than they expected, and nearly 1 in 10 have gone into debt to cover their pet's care. The charity has coined a term for it: "petflation."

The good news is that medicine prices aren't random — and once you understand what actually drives them, you're in a much stronger position to pay less without cutting corners on your pet's health.

What Actually Makes the Same Medicine Cost Different Prices?

1. Where the Prescription Was Written

Prices for veterinary consultations — and the prescription fee that often comes with them — vary significantly depending on the type of practice. Recent industry data puts the average UK vet consultation at around £58, but the range across the country runs from roughly £30 to over £75, with practices in large corporate groups typically charging more than independent practices.

2. The Prescription Fee Itself

Getting a written prescription so you can buy medicine elsewhere isn't always free. The Competition and Markets Authority (CMA) found that some practices have been charging £30 or more just to issue a prescription — before the cost of the medicine is even added.

3. Brand Name vs Active Ingredient

This is the one most owners don't know to check. Many veterinary medicines are sold under a branded name, but the active ingredient — the part that actually treats your pet — is often available under multiple names, sometimes at very different prices. A product isn't automatically "better" because it costs more; it needs to contain the same active ingredient, at the same dose, to do the same job.

4. Regional Pricing

Location plays a bigger role than most owners assume. Practices in London and the South East regularly charge a noticeable premium over the UK average, purely due to higher operating costs in those areas.

The CMA's 2026 Ruling: What's Actually Changing

This isn't just background context — it's directly relevant to every UK pet owner right now. After a two-year investigation into the veterinary sector, the CMA published its final decision report in March 2026, concluding that the market wasn't working as well as it should for consumers, with pet owners often left in the dark about pricing.

Some of the key changes now legally required of veterinary practices include:

  • A cap on prescription fees — up to £21 for the first medicine and £12.50 for each additional one, so practices can no longer charge whatever they like just to let you shop elsewhere
  • Mandatory published price lists on practice websites — the CMA found fewer than 40% of practices currently list their prices clearly
  • Written estimates for any treatment expected to cost £500 or more
  • Clearer information about whether a practice is independently owned or part of a large corporate group, since the CMA found the latter tend to charge more on average

These measures are being phased in through 2026 and 2027, but the direction of travel is clear: UK pet owners are gaining a legal right to clearer pricing and easier price comparison — which is precisely the gap Maggie Search exists to close.

How to Legally Pay Less for Your Pet's Medicine

  1. Always ask for a written prescription. Your vet is required to provide one if you ask, and under the CMA's new rules, the fee for it is capped.
  2. Check the active ingredient, not just the brand name, before assuming a cheaper option is inferior — ask your vet or pharmacist to confirm it's an equivalent product at the correct dose for your pet.
  3. Compare registered sellers. Only buy from veterinary retailers registered with the Veterinary Medicines Directorate (VMD) — this is non-negotiable for your pet's safety, not just your wallet.
  4. Never substitute human medication. PDSA's research found that a worrying number of owners have considered giving pets human medicines to save money — this can be extremely dangerous, as dosing and toxicity differ significantly between species.

This article is for general educational purposes and does not replace advice from a registered veterinary surgeon. Always consult your vet before starting, stopping, or switching your pet's medication, and never give your pet human medication without professional guidance.

The Bottom Line

Medicine price differences aren't random, and they're not something you have to simply accept. Between rising "petflation" pressures and a regulator now legally forcing more transparency into the system, UK pet owners have more power to compare and save than ever before — they just need the right information to act on it.
